Question:
The points are defined to be collinear if
\begin{align}
	\rank\myvec{1&1&1\\\vec{A}&\vec{B}&\vec{C}} = 2
\end{align}
Are the points collinear?\\
\fi
\solution
Given in the question:
\begin{align}
    \vec{A} &= \myvec{ 1 \\ -1 }\\
    \vec{B} &= \myvec{ -4 \\ 6 }\\
    \vec{C} &= \myvec{ -3 \\ -5 } 
\end{align}
\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{solutions/1/1/3/figs/fig.png}
\caption{Proof that A, B and C or non-collinear}
\label{fig:}
\end{figure}
\begin{align}
	\rank\myvec{1&1&1\\\vec{A}&\vec{B}&\vec{C}} &= \rank\myvec{1&1&1\\-1&-4&-3\\1&6&-5}
\end{align}
Solving by row-echelon method,
\begin{align}
	\myvec{1&1&1\\1&-4&-3\\1&6&-5} \xleftrightarrow[]{R_2 \leftarrow R_2-R_1}& \myvec{1&1&1\\0&-5&-4\\1&6&-5}\\
	\xleftrightarrow[]{R_3 \leftarrow R_3-R_1}& \myvec{1&1&1\\0&-5&-4\\0&7&-4}\\
	\xleftrightarrow[]{R_3 \leftarrow R_3+\frac{7}{5}R_2}& \myvec{1&1&1\\0&-5&-4\\0&0&\frac{-48}{5}}
\end{align}
$\therefore$ rank of matrix = number of non-zero rows = 3. Hence the points are not collinear
